Chapter 1, Run

This chapter describes how Black South Africans were divided into tribes before apartheid
officially segregated South Africa by race. The Zulu and Xhosa tribes had a long history of conflict,
with the Zulu resisting European invaders with force and the Xhosa seeking to learn
from Europeans and strategize against their invasion.

Trevor's mother, a devout Christian, took her family to various churches catering
to different racial demographics, facing logistical challenges along the way.
Her unwavering faith and courage saved Trevor's life when a menacing bus driver threatened them.
This chapter highlights the racial tensions and resilience of South Africans during apartheid.

Chapter 2,Born a Crime

This chapter delves into the complexities of being mixed-race during apartheid.
Trevor was born during this period,with his mother being Black (Xhosa) and his father white.
Sexual relationships between races were illegal, and many mixed-race children were born.

The Afrikaners, who took over the South African government after British rule ended,
formalized an advanced system of racial oppression known as apartheid. While South Africa's
transition from apartheid overshadowed the Rwandan genocide, both countries grappled with
ethnic divisions and violence. The Rwandan Patriotic Front (RPF),mainly Tutsi refugees,
invaded Rwanda from Uganda, leading to tensions and massacres.

As a Rwandan, I can relate to the themes of discrimination, ethnic tensions, and the resilience
of ordinary people depicted in "Born a Crime." Our tragic past, including the 1994 genocide,
shares similarities with the struggles of South Africans during apartheid. Trevor's narrative is a
reminder that understanding our shared histories can promote empathy and foster healing across borders.
